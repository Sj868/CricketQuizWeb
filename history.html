History of Legendary Cricketers

Virat Kohli

Born on November 5, 1988, in Delhi, India, Virat Kohli is regarded as one of the greatest batsmen in cricket history. He led the Indian U-19 team to victory in the 2008 World Cup and made his ODI debut later that year against Sri Lanka. Kohli is the first player to feature in 300 ODIs, 100 Tests, and 100 T20Is, achieving this milestone during the 2025 ICC Champions Trophy. He holds the record for the most ODI centuries (51 as of 2025) and is the fastest to reach 14,000 ODI runs (299 innings). Kohli captained India to a historic Test series win in Australia in 2018-19 and has won multiple ICC awards, including Cricketer of the Decade (2020).

Sachin Tendulkar

Known as the 'God of Cricket,' Sachin Tendulkar was born on April 24, 1973, in Mumbai, India. He debuted for India in 1989 at age 16 against Pakistan, becoming the youngest Indian to play internationally. Tendulkar holds the record for the most international runs (34,357) and was the first to score 100 international centuries (retiring with 100 in 2013). He played 463 ODIs, the most by any Indian, and was a key part of India’s 2011 World Cup-winning team. Tendulkar’s 24-year career ended in 2013, leaving a legacy of technical brilliance and humility.

Sir Don Bradman

Born on August 27, 1908, in New South Wales, Australia, Sir Don Bradman is often hailed as the greatest batsman ever. His Test career spanned 1928 to 1948, where he scored 6,996 runs in 52 matches at an astonishing average of 99.94, a record unlikely to be broken. Bradman scored 29 centuries, including two triple centuries, and led Australia to multiple Ashes victories. His disciplined technique and mental fortitude redefined batting standards. Bradman passed away in 2001, but his legacy endures as a benchmark for cricketing excellence.

Sir Vivian Richards

Born on March 7, 1952, in Antigua, Sir Vivian Richards was a dominant force in cricket during the 1970s and 1980s. Known for his aggressive batting, Richards scored 8,540 Test runs at an average of 50.23 and 6,721 ODI runs. He never wore a helmet, yet intimidated bowlers with his fearless approach. Richards led the West Indies to the 1979 World Cup title and was part of the 1975 winning team. His 189* against England in 1984 remains one of the finest ODI innings. Richards’ swagger and power left an indelible mark on the game.

Brian Lara

Born on May 2, 1969, in Trinidad, Brian Lara is celebrated for his elegant batting and record-breaking feats. He holds the highest individual Test score of 400* against England in 2004, surpassing his own 375 set in 1994. Lara scored 11,953 Test runs and 10,405 ODI runs, with 53 international centuries. His 169 against Sri Lanka in 1995 helped the West Indies chase a then-record 343 in an ODI. Lara’s ability to play long, match-defining innings made him a West Indian icon, retiring in 2007 as one of the game’s finest.

Shane Warne

Born on September 13, 1969, in Victoria, Australia, Shane Warne revolutionized spin bowling. Debuting in 1992, Warne took 708 Test wickets, the second-most ever, and 293 ODI wickets. His ‘Ball of the Century’ in 1993 dismissed Mike Gatting and marked his arrival. Warne was a key part of Australia’s 1999 World Cup win and led them to multiple Ashes triumphs. Known for his charisma and tactical acumen, Warne’s 145 Test matches included 37 five-wicket hauls. He passed away in 2022, leaving a legacy as one of cricket’s greatest entertainers.

Muttiah Muralitharan

Born on April 17, 1972, in Kandy, Sri Lanka, Muttiah Muralitharan is widely regarded as the greatest Test bowler ever. He holds the record for the most wickets in international cricket with 1,347 (800 in Tests, 534 in ODIs). Debuting in 1992, Murali’s unique wrist-spinning action baffled batsmen worldwide. He is the only player to take 10 wickets in four consecutive Tests and 50+ wickets against every Test nation. Muralitharan played a pivotal role in Sri Lanka’s 1996 World Cup win and retired in 2011 as a spin-bowling legend.

Sir Garfield Sobers

Born on July 28, 1936, in Barbados, Sir Garfield Sobers is considered the most complete cricketer ever. A brilliant all-rounder, Sobers scored 8,032 Test runs at an average of 57.78 and took 235 wickets with his versatile bowling (left-arm pace and spin). He holds the record for the highest Test score at the time, an unbeaten 365 against Pakistan in 1958, and was the first to hit six sixes in an over in first-class cricket. Sobers’ elegance and adaptability made him a West Indian icon until his retirement in 1974.

Wasim Akram

Born on June 3, 1966, in Lahore, Pakistan, Wasim Akram, known as the ‘Sultan of Swing,’ redefined fast bowling. Debuting in 1984, Akram took 414 Test wickets and 502 ODI wickets, becoming the first to reach 500 ODI wickets. His mastery of reverse swing and in-swinging yorkers made him a nightmare for batsmen. Akram played a key role in Pakistan’s 1992 World Cup victory and holds the record for most wickets in List A cricket (881). Retiring in 2003, Akram remains one of cricket’s greatest fast bowlers.

Kumar Sangakkara

Born on October 27, 1977, in Matale, Sri Lanka, Kumar Sangakkara is one of the finest wicketkeeper-batsmen in cricket history. He scored 12,400 Test runs at an average of 57.40 and 14,234 ODI runs, one of the few to score 10,000+ runs in both formats. Sangakkara’s elegant stroke play and sharp keeping earned him the ICC Cricketer of the Year award in 2012. A key member of Sri Lanka’s 2014 T20 World Cup-winning team, he retired in 2015 and remains a respected figure in cricket governance.

Sunil Gavaskar

Born on July 10, 1949, in Mumbai, India, Sunil Gavaskar, known as the ‘Little Master,’ was a batting pioneer. Debuting in 1971, he became the first to score 10,000 Test runs, finishing with 10,122 at an average of 51.12. Facing the fearsome West Indies pacers without a helmet, Gavaskar scored 13 centuries against them, a record. His technique and focus made him India’s first global batting star. Retiring in 1987, Gavaskar played a key role in establishing India as a competitive cricketing nation.

Imran Khan

Born on October 5, 1952, in Lahore, Pakistan, Imran Khan was a transformative all-rounder and captain. He scored 3,807 Test runs and took 362 wickets, averaging 50 with the bat and 19 with the ball in his final decade. Khan led Pakistan to the 1992 World Cup title, a defining moment in the nation’s cricketing history. His revolutionary fast bowling and leadership inspired generations. Retiring in 1992, Khan’s legacy as one of cricket’s greatest all-rounders endures.

AB de Villiers

Born on February 17, 1984, in Pretoria, South Africa, AB de Villiers, nicknamed ‘Mr. 360,’ redefined batting with his versatility. He scored 8,765 Test runs at an average of 50.66 and 9,577 ODI runs at 53.50. De Villiers holds the record for the fastest ODI century (31 balls) against West Indies in 2015. A phenomenal fielder and occasional wicketkeeper, he led South Africa in all formats before retiring in 2018. De Villiers’ innovative shot-making made him a fan favorite worldwide.

MS Dhoni

Born on July 7, 1981, in Ranchi, India, MS Dhoni, known as ‘Captain Cool,’ is one of cricket’s greatest leaders. He scored 4,876 Test runs and 10,773 ODI runs, averaging 50.57 in ODIs. Dhoni led India to the 2007 T20 World Cup, 2011 Cricket World Cup, and 2013 Champions Trophy, the only captain to win all three ICC trophies. His iconic six to win the 2011 World Cup final is legendary. Retiring in 2020, Dhoni’s calm demeanor and finishing skills remain unmatched.

Kapil Dev

Born on January 6, 1959, in Chandigarh, India, Kapil Dev is one of India’s greatest all-rounders. He scored 5,248 Test runs and took 434 wickets, plus 3,783 runs and 253 wickets in ODIs. Kapil led India to its first World Cup victory in 1983, famously scoring 175* against Zimbabwe. He holds the record for the fastest Test century by an Indian (74 balls). Retiring in 1994, Kapil’s legacy as a trailblazer for Indian cricket endures.

Jacques Kallis

Born on October 16, 1975, in Cape Town, South Africa, Jacques Kallis is regarded as cricket’s greatest all-rounder. He scored 13,289 Test runs at an average of 55.37 and took 292 wickets, plus 11,579 ODI runs and 200 wickets. Kallis is the only player with 10,000+ runs and 200+ wickets in both Tests and ODIs. A key part of South Africa’s dominance, he retired in 2014 with 45 Test centuries, leaving a legacy of consistency and versatility.

Ricky Ponting

Born on December 19, 1974, in Tasmania, Australia, Ricky Ponting is one of Australia’s most successful captains. He scored 13,378 Test runs at an average of 51.85 and 13,704 ODI runs, with 71 international centuries. Ponting led Australia to World Cup victories in 2003 and 2007, and holds the record for most Test wins as captain (48). His iconic pull shot and leadership defined an era. Retiring in 2012, Ponting remains a cricketing legend.

Adam Gilchrist

Born on November 14, 1971, in New South Wales, Australia, Adam Gilchrist revolutionized the role of wicketkeeper-batsmen. He scored 5,570 Test runs at an average of 47.60 and 9,619 ODI runs, with a strike rate of 96.94 in ODIs. Gilchrist’s aggressive batting, including a 57-ball Test century against England in 2006, changed the game. A key part of Australia’s 1999, 2003, and 2007 World Cup wins, he retired in 2008 as one of cricket’s most impactful players.

Allan Border

Born on July 27, 1955, in Sydney, Australia, Allan Border was the backbone of Australian cricket in the 1980s. He scored 11,174 Test runs at an average of 50.56, with 27 centuries. As captain, Border transformed Australia into a dominant force, leading them to the 1987 World Cup title. He held the record for most Test matches played (156) at retirement in 1994. The Allan Border Medal, awarded to Australia’s best player annually, honors his legacy.

Virender Sehwag

Born on October 20, 1978, in Delhi, India, Virender Sehwag redefined opening batting with his fearless approach. He scored 8,586 Test runs at an average of 49.34, including two triple centuries (319 vs South Africa, 2008). Sehwag’s aggressive style yielded 23 Test centuries and a 219 in ODIs, the second-highest ODI score at the time. His strike rate of over 80 in Tests is remarkable. Retiring in 2015, Sehwag remains an Indian cricket icon.

Malcolm Marshall

Born on April 18, 1958, in Barbados, Malcolm Marshall was one of the greatest fast bowlers ever. He took 376 Test wickets at an average of 20.94, the best among bowlers with 200+ wickets. Marshall’s lethal bouncer, in-swinger, and leg-cutter made him a key part of the West Indies’ pace attack in the 1980s. He also scored 10 Test fifties as a lower-order batsman. Marshall passed away in 1999, leaving a legacy of pace and precision.

Sir Ian Botham

Born on November 24, 1955, in Cheshire, England, Sir Ian Botham was a game-changing all-rounder. He scored 5,200 Test runs and took 383 wickets, including 14 five-wicket hauls. Botham’s heroics in the 1981 Ashes, dubbed ‘Botham’s Ashes,’ included a 149* at Headingley that turned the series. He also took 145 ODI wickets. Retiring in 1992, Botham’s flair and match-winning ability inspired generations of English cricketers.

Chris Gayle

Born on September 21, 1979, in Kingston, Jamaica, Chris Gayle, the ‘Universe Boss,’ is a T20 legend. He scored 7,214 Test runs and 10,480 ODI runs, but his 12,528 T20 runs are unmatched. Gayle is one of four players with two Test triple centuries (317 vs South Africa, 2005) and holds the record for most sixes in international cricket (553). His 175* in IPL 2013 is the highest T20 score. Retiring in 2021, Gayle redefined power-hitting.

Shoaib Akhtar

Born on August 13, 1975, in Rawalpindi, Pakistan, Shoaib Akhtar, the ‘Rawalpindi Express,’ was one of the fastest bowlers ever. He delivered the fastest recorded ball at 161.3 km/h in the 2003 World Cup. Akhtar took 178 Test wickets and 247 ODI wickets, often dismantling batting lineups with raw pace. His eight-wicket haul in the 1999 Asian Test Championship, dismissing Dravid and Tendulkar in successive deliveries, is iconic. Akhtar retired in 2011.

Shahid Afridi

Born on March 1, 1980, in Khyber, Pakistan, Shahid Afridi, known as ‘Boom Boom,’ was a dynamic all-rounder. He holds the record for the fastest ODI century (37 balls vs Sri Lanka, 1996) and scored 32 runs in a single over. Afridi hit 351 international sixes and took 395 ODI wickets, the second-most in T20Is at retirement in 2017. His aggressive style and match-winning flair, including a 2009 T20 World Cup semi-final 54*, made him a Pakistani icon.

Sir Richard Hadlee

Born on July 3, 1951, in Christchurch, New Zealand, Sir Richard Hadlee was a fast-bowling genius. He took 431 Test wickets, a record at the time, at an average of 22.29. Hadlee was the first to reach 400 Test wickets and scored 3,124 runs as a handy batsman. His 9/52 against Australia in 1985 remains one of the best bowling figures in Tests. Retiring in 1990, Hadlee elevated New Zealand’s cricketing stature single-handedly.

Matthew Hayden

Born on October 29, 1971, in Queensland, Australia, Matthew Hayden was a dominant Test opener. He scored 8,625 Test runs at an average of 50.73, with 30 centuries, including a then-record 380 against Zimbabwe in 2003. Hayden’s powerful strokeplay was key to Australia’s golden era, contributing to the 1999, 2003, and 2007 World Cup wins. He also scored 6,133 ODI runs. Retiring in 2009, Hayden’s imposing presence at the crease is legendary.

Wally Hammond

Born on June 19, 1903, in Dover, England, Wally Hammond was one of England’s finest batsmen. He scored 7,249 Test runs at an average of 58.45, with 22 centuries, and took 83 wickets. Hammond’s elegant technique shone in his 336* against New Zealand in 1933, a Test record at the time. He played 85 Tests from 1927 to 1947, excelling in all conditions. Hammond passed away in 1965, leaving a legacy of grace and consistency.

Sir Jack Hobbs

Born on December 16, 1882, in Cambridge, England, Sir Jack Hobbs, known as ‘The Master,’ was a batting pioneer. He scored 5,410 Test runs at an average of 56.94, with 15 centuries, and 61,760 first-class runs, a record. Hobbs was the first cricketer knighted by the queen in 1953. Playing from 1908 to 1930, he mastered all conditions, especially against Australia. Hobbs passed away in 1963, revered as one of cricket’s greatest openers.

Rahul Dravid

Born on January 11, 1973, in Indore, India, Rahul Dravid, known as ‘The Wall,’ was a batting rock. He scored 13,288 Test runs at an average of 52.31, with 36 centuries, and 10,889 ODI runs. Dravid holds the record for most catches in Tests (210) as a fielder. His 270 against Pakistan in 2004 helped India win a historic series. Retiring in 2012, Dravid’s technique and resilience made him a cornerstone of Indian cricket.

Glenn McGrath

Born on February 9, 1970, in New South Wales, Australia, Glenn McGrath was a fast-bowling metronome. He took 563 Test wickets at an average of 21.64 and 381 ODI wickets, playing a key role in Australia’s 1999, 2003, and 2007 World Cup wins. McGrath’s 7/15 against Namibia in 2003 is the best World Cup bowling figure. His relentless accuracy tormented batsmen. Retiring in 2007, McGrath remains one of cricket’s greatest pacers.

Steve Waugh

Born on June 2, 1965, in Sydney, Australia, Steve Waugh was a gritty batsman and astute captain. He scored 10,927 Test runs at an average of 51.06, with 32 centuries, and 7,569 ODI runs. Waugh led Australia to the 1999 World Cup title and 16 consecutive Test wins, a record. His 120* against South Africa in 1999 turned the tide in the World Cup. Retiring in 2004, Waugh’s leadership and resilience defined Australian cricket.

Waqar Younis

Born on November 16, 1971, in Vehari, Pakistan, Waqar Younis was a reverse-swing maestro. He took 373 Test wickets and 416 ODI wickets, with his 7/36 against England in 1990 being a highlight. Waqar’s toe-crushing yorkers and pace made him a feared bowler. He holds the record for most five-wicket hauls in ODIs (13, tied). Retiring in 2003, Waqar’s partnership with Wasim Akram formed one of cricket’s deadliest bowling duos.

Curtly Ambrose

Born on September 21, 1963, in Antigua, Curtly Ambrose was a towering West Indian fast bowler. He took 405 Test wickets at an average of 20.99 and 225 ODI wickets. Ambrose’s 7/1 in a 1993 Test spell against Australia decimated their batting. Standing 6’7”, his bounce and accuracy were lethal. A key part of the West Indies’ dominance in the 1990s, Ambrose retired in 2000 as one of the game’s most intimidating bowlers.

Anil Kumble

Born on October 17, 1970, in Bangalore, India, Anil Kumble was a leg-spin wizard. He took 619 Test wickets, the third-most ever, including a perfect 10/74 against Pakistan in 1999, one of only two such feats in Test history. Kumble also scored 2,506 Test runs. His 337 ODI wickets include a 6/12 against West Indies in 1993. Retiring in 2008, Kumble’s determination and guile made him India’s greatest match-winner.

Clive Lloyd

Born on August 31, 1944, in Georgetown, Guyana, Clive Lloyd was a visionary West Indian captain. He scored 7,515 Test runs at an average of 46.67 and led the West Indies to World Cup wins in 1975 and 1979. Lloyd’s 102 in the 1975 World Cup final set the tone for West Indian dominance. His leadership united a fearsome pace attack, making the West Indies unbeatable in the 1970s and 1980s. Lloyd retired in 1985.

Greg Chappell

Born on August 7, 1948, in Adelaide, Australia, Greg Chappell was a batting maestro. He scored 7,110 Test runs at an average of 53.86, with 24 centuries, including 247* against New Zealand in 1974. Chappell’s elegant drives and cover shots were textbook. He also took 47 Test wickets and captained Australia to 21 Test wins. Retiring in 1984, Chappell’s influence as a batsman and coach shaped Australian cricket for decades.

Dennis Lillee

Born on July 18, 1949, in Perth, Australia, Dennis Lillee was a fast-bowling icon. He took 355 Test wickets at an average of 23.92, a record at the time, and 103 ODI wickets. Lillee’s fiery pace and aggression, exemplified by his 7/83 against West Indies in 1971, terrorized batsmen. He played a key role in Australia’s 1970s resurgence. Retiring in 1984, Lillee’s legacy as one of cricket’s fiercest competitors endures.

Javed Miandad

Born on June 12, 1957, in Karachi, Pakistan, Javed Miandad was a batting genius. He scored 8,832 Test runs at an average of 52.57 and 7,381 ODI runs. Miandad’s last-ball six against India in 1986 to win the Austral-Asia Cup is iconic. He played 124 Tests, scoring 23 centuries, and was a master of pressure situations. Retiring in 1996, Miandad’s street-smart batting and competitive spirit made him a Pakistani legend.

Michael Holding

Born on February 16, 1954, in Kingston, Jamaica, Michael Holding, known as ‘Whispering Death,’ was a West Indian pace legend. He took 249 Test wickets at an average of 23.68, with his 14/149 against England in 1976 being the best match figures by a West Indian. Holding’s smooth run-up and lethal pace defined the West Indies’ dominance. Retiring in 1987, he later became a respected commentator, known for his insightful analysis.

Sir Alastair Cook

Born on December 25, 1984, in Gloucester, England, Sir Alastair Cook is England’s leading Test run-scorer. He amassed 12,472 runs at an average of 45.35, with 33 centuries, including 294 against India in 2011. Cook captained England to Ashes victories in 2013 and 2015 and holds the record for most Test matches played by an Englishman (159). Retiring in 2018, Cook’s resilience and consistency made him a modern great.

James Anderson

Born on July 30, 1982, in Burnley, England, James Anderson is Test cricket’s most successful fast bowler. He took 704 Test wickets, the most by a pacer, at an average of 26.45, and holds the record for most maidens bowled in Tests (1,288). Anderson’s swing bowling, highlighted by his 7/42 against West Indies in 2017, was unplayable. Retiring in 2024, Anderson’s longevity and skill made him an English icon.

Graeme Smith

Born on February 1, 1981, in Johannesburg, South Africa, Graeme Smith was a prolific opener and captain. He scored 9,265 Test runs at an average of 48.25, with 27 centuries, including back-to-back double centuries (277 and 259) against England in 2003. Smith led South Africa in a record 109 Tests, guiding them to the No. 1 ranking. Retiring in 2014, Smith’s leadership and grit made him a South African hero.

Inzamam-ul-Haq

Born on March 3, 1970, in Multan, Pakistan, Inzamam-ul-Haq was a batting powerhouse. He scored 8,830 Test runs at an average of 49.60 and 11,739 ODI runs, with 25 Test centuries. His 60 in the 1992 World Cup semi-final against New Zealand was match-defining. Inzamam captained Pakistan to the 2007 T20 World Cup final. Retiring in 2007, his calm presence and powerful strokeplay made him a Pakistani legend.

Andy Roberts

Born on January 29, 1951, in Antigua, Andy Roberts was a pioneer of West Indian pace bowling. He took 202 Test wickets at an average of 25.61, with his 7/54 against India in 1974 being a standout. Roberts’ intelligent variations and bounce were key to the West Indies’ dominance in the 1970s. He also took 87 ODI wickets, including in the 1975 World Cup final. Retiring in 1983, Roberts set the stage for future pace bowlers.

Gordon Greenidge

Born on May 1, 1951, in Barbados, Gordon Greenidge was a fearsome West Indian opener. He scored 7,558 Test runs at an average of 44.72, with 19 centuries, including 214* in the 1976 Lord’s Test against England. Greenidge’s 327-run opening stand with Desmond Haynes against England in 1990 is a record. His aggressive batting helped the West Indies win the 1979 World Cup. Retiring in 1991, Greenidge was a batting cornerstone.

Desmond Haynes

Born on February 15, 1956, in Barbados, Desmond Haynes formed a legendary opening pair with Gordon Greenidge. He scored 7,487 Test runs at an average of 42.29 and 8,648 ODI runs, with 26 ODI centuries, a record at the time. Haynes’ 148 in the 1979 World Cup final was pivotal. His 26 Test century partnerships with Greenidge are unmatched. Retiring in 1994, Haynes’ consistency made him a West Indian great.

Courtney Walsh

Born on October 30, 1962, in Kingston, Jamaica, Courtney Walsh was a West Indian pace giant. He took 519 Test wickets at an average of 24.44, the first to reach 500 Test wickets, and 227 ODI wickets. Walsh’s 5/1 spell against Sri Lanka in 1986 is legendary. He captained the West Indies in 22 Tests. Retiring in 2001, Walsh’s endurance and partnership with Ambrose made him a fearsome bowler.

Bob Willis

Born on May 30, 1949, in Sunderland, England, Bob Willis was a fast-bowling stalwart. He took 325 Test wickets at an average of 25.20, with his 8/43 in the 1981 Ashes at Headingley turning the series. Willis played 90 Tests and was England’s leading wicket-taker until 1984. His fiery pace and determination were key in the 1970s and 1980s. Willis passed away in 2019, remembered as an Ashes hero.

Fred Trueman

Born on February 6, 1931, in Yorkshire, England, Fred Trueman, known as ‘Fiery Fred,’ was a fast-bowling legend. He took 307 Test wickets at an average of 21.57, the first to reach 300 Test wickets. Trueman’s 8/31 against India in 1952 showcased his pace and swing. Playing from 1952 to 1965, he was a key part of England’s attack. Trueman passed away in 2006, leaving a legacy of fiery competitiveness.

Sir Len Hutton

Born on June 23, 1916, in Yorkshire, England, Sir Len Hutton was a batting great. He scored 6,971 Test runs at an average of 56.67, with 19 centuries, including a record 364 against Australia in 1938, the highest Test score until 1958. Hutton played 79 Tests from 1937 to 1955 and captained England to reclaim the Ashes in 1953. Hutton passed away in 1990, remembered for his technique and resilience.

Mark Waugh

Born on June 2, 1965, in Sydney, Australia, Mark Waugh was an elegant batsman and brilliant fielder. He scored 8,029 Test runs at an average of 41.81, with 20 centuries, and 8,500 ODI runs. Waugh’s 173 against West Indies in 1996 was a masterclass. He took 181 Test catches, a record for a non-wicketkeeper at the time. Retiring in 2002, Waugh’s grace and slip fielding made him a fan favorite.

Ian Bell

Born on April 11, 1982, in Coventry, England, Ian Bell was a stylish batsman. He scored 7,727 Test runs at an average of 42.69, with 22 centuries, including 144 in the 2013 Ashes. Bell’s cover drives were iconic, earning praise from Ricky Ponting. He played 118 Tests and was part of England’s 2013 and 2015 Ashes wins. Retiring in 2015, Bell’s elegance and consistency made him a modern English great.

VVS Laxman

Born on November 1, 1974, in Hyderabad, India, VVS Laxman was a wristy batsman. He scored 8,781 Test runs at an average of 45.97, with 17 centuries, including a 281 against Australia in 2001, one of the greatest Test innings. Laxman’s partnerships with Dravid were legendary, often rescuing India. He also scored 2,338 ODI runs. Retiring in 2012, Laxman’s artistry against spin and pace made him a clutch performer.

Zaheer Khan

Born on October 7, 1978, in Maharashtra, India, Zaheer Khan was a crafty left-arm pacer. He took 311 Test wickets at an average of 32.94 and 282 ODI wickets, playing a key role in India’s 2011 World Cup win. Zaheer’s 5/29 against New Zealand in 2000 announced his arrival. His reverse swing and tactical nous were vital for India. Retiring in 2015, Zaheer remains one of India’s best fast bowlers.

Saeed Anwar

Born on September 6, 1968, in Karachi, Pakistan, Saeed Anwar was a stylish opener. He scored 4,052 Test runs at an average of 45.52 and 8,824 ODI runs, with 20 ODI centuries, a record at the time. Anwar’s 194 against India in 1997 was the highest ODI score then. His elegant drives and timing were a treat. Retiring in 2003, Anwar’s 1999 World Cup performances cemented his legacy as a Pakistani great.

Sourav Ganguly

Born on July 8, 1972, in Kolkata, India, Sourav Ganguly, the ‘Prince of Calcutta,’ was a transformative captain. He scored 7,212 Test runs and 11,363 ODI runs, with 22 ODI centuries. Ganguly’s 183 in the 1999 World Cup is one of the best ODI knocks. He led India to the 2003 World Cup final and instilled aggression in the team. Retiring in 2008, Ganguly’s leadership reshaped Indian cricket.

Shivnarine Chanderpaul

Born on August 16, 1974, in Guyana, Shivnarine Chanderpaul was a resilient West Indian batsman. He scored 11,867 Test runs at an average of 51.37, with 30 centuries, and 8,778 ODI runs. Chanderpaul’s unorthodox stance and gritty 153* against Australia in 2008 were highlights. He played 164 Tests, a West Indian record. Retiring in 2015, Chanderpaul’s consistency kept the West Indies competitive during a tough era.

Mark Boucher

Born on December 3, 1976, in East London, South Africa, Mark Boucher was a record-breaking wicketkeeper. He holds the Test record for most dismissals (555) and scored 5,515 runs at an average of 30.30. Boucher’s 147* against Zimbabwe in 1999 showed his batting prowess. He played 147 Tests and was part of South Africa’s 1998 Champions Trophy win. A 2012 eye injury forced his retirement, ending a stellar career.

Sanath Jayasuriya

Born on June 30, 1969, in Matara, Sri Lanka, Sanath Jayasuriya revolutionized ODI batting. He scored 13,430 ODI runs, with 28 centuries, and 6,973 Test runs. Jayasuriya’s 340 against India in 1997 is one of the highest Test scores. His explosive 189 in the 2000 Champions Trophy final was pivotal for Sri Lanka. Also taking 323 ODI wickets, he retired in 2011 as a game-changer for limited-overs cricket.

Mahela Jayawardene

Born on May 27, 1977, in Colombo, Sri Lanka, Mahela Jayawardene was a batting artist. He scored 11,814 Test runs at an average of 49.84, with 34 centuries, including 374 against South Africa in 2006, a Sri Lankan record. Jayawardene also scored 12,650 ODI runs. He led Sri Lanka to the 2014 T20 World Cup title. Retiring in 2014, Jayawardene’s elegance and leadership made him a Sri Lankan icon.

Aravinda de Silva

Born on October 17, 1965, in Colombo, Sri Lanka, Aravinda de Silva was a match-winner. He scored 6,361 Test runs at an average of 42.97 and 9,284 ODI runs. De Silva’s unbeaten 107 in the 1996 World Cup final against Australia, along with 3/42, won Sri Lanka the title. His 167* in a 1997 Test against Pakistan was equally brilliant. Retiring in 2003, de Silva’s flair and clutch performances are legendary.

Brendon McCullum

Born on September 27, 1981, in Dunedin, New Zealand, Brendon McCullum was an aggressive batsman. He scored 6,453 Test runs, including a 302 against India in 2014, the first New Zealand Test triple century, and 6,083 ODI runs. McCullum’s 77-ball 123 in the 2012 T20 World Cup is iconic. He led New Zealand to the 2015 World Cup final. Retiring in 2016, McCullum’s fearless approach transformed New Zealand cricket.

Kane Williamson

Born on August 8, 1990, in Tauranga, New Zealand, Kane Williamson is a modern batting great. As of 2025, he has scored over 8,500 Test runs at an average of 55.00, with 29 centuries, and 6,810 ODI runs. Williamson’s 148 in the 2019 World Cup semi-final against South Africa was a masterclass. He led New Zealand to the 2021 World Test Championship title. Williamson’s technique and leadership make him a Kiwi icon.

Shakib Al Hasan

Born on March 24, 1987, in Magura, Bangladesh, Shakib Al Hasan is a world-class all-rounder. As of 2025, he has scored 4,505 Test runs and taken 242 wickets, plus 7,570 ODI runs and 312 wickets. Shakib’s 606 runs in the 2019 World Cup are a record for an all-rounder in a single tournament. He has been ranked the No. 1 all-rounder across formats for years. Shakib’s versatility has elevated Bangladesh cricket.

Ben Stokes
